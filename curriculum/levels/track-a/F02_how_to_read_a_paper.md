## Why this level matters (lineage)

**Classical root:** close reading — the humanities tradition of reading the same passage three times at different depths.
**Modern descendant:** Keshav's 2007 paper "How to Read a Paper" adapted this for CS. Every DL researcher alive has read one or the other.

## Objectives

- Learn the three passes: skim, content, critical.
- Apply all three to a real AI paper.

## Resources

- [Keshav, "How to Read a Paper" (2 pages, PDF)](https://web.stanford.edu/class/ee384m/Handouts/HowtoReadPaper.pdf)
- Paper to practice on: Karpathy's "Software 2.0" Medium post (short, opinionated, foundational).

## Tasks

- [ ] Read Keshav's paper. Takes ~10 minutes.
- [ ] Apply the three-pass method to Karpathy's "Software 2.0". Spend no more than 45 minutes total.
- [ ] Write a one-paragraph summary in `notes/F02.md` capturing: what's the paper's main claim, what evidence, what would you critique.

## Done criteria

You can explain the three passes from memory and have produced a short written summary of "Software 2.0".

## Luminary spotlight — S. Keshav (b. 1965)

Professor of Computer Science at the University of Cambridge (previously Waterloo), known for networking research (TCP/IP, content-centric networking) but cited ten times more often for a one-page CV aside: his 2007 note "How to Read a Paper". He wrote it to help his own students and ended up providing the default mental model for a generation of engineers learning to navigate the research literature. The method works well precisely because it sets expectations — a first pass *should* feel shallow.
